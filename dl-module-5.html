More Complex Models — the fundamentals

The standard image network is powerful, but it assumes a fixed-size, single snapshot of fixed structure. The wider world isn't always like that: some data unfolds over time, some comes as sequences of arbitrary length, and sometimes the goal isn't to recognise something but to create something new. The models here extend the basics to handle exactly those cases.

Example
A single photo of a person mid-stride doesn't tell you if they're walking or stumbling — you need the sequence of frames. A sentence isn't a fixed-size grid — it's a stream of words whose length varies. And "draw a new face that has never existed" isn't recognition at all. Each needs a model built for that shape of problem.

When the third dimension is time

An ordinary image filter scans height and width. When data has a meaningful third axis — frames of a video over time, or slices of a 3-D scan through depth — the filter can be extended to scan that axis too, capturing how things change across it.

Example
Flipping through a stack of flip-book pages instead of staring at one: only by scanning across the pages (the time axis) do you see the figure move. The cost is real, though — processing a stack is far heavier than glancing at a single page, so this power is worth it only when the extra axis truly carries signal.

When data is a sequence

Text, speech, and time series are sequences: order matters and length varies. Two broad approaches handle them. One reads the sequence step by step, carrying a running memory of what came before. The other lets every element look directly at every other element at once — a mechanism called attention.

Example
Reading a mystery novel, you carry the plot forward in your head as you go (step-by-step memory). But to catch that "the butler" on page 300 refers to someone introduced on page 3, it helps to glance straight back at page 3 rather than relying on a fading memory — that direct glance across any distance is attention.

The challenge sequences create

Both approaches have a characteristic cost:

  • Step-by-step memory fades over long distances. Information from far back can wash out before it's needed — the difficulty of long-range dependencies.
  • "Everyone looks at everyone" gets expensive fast. If every element attends to every other, the work grows with the square of the sequence length, so very long inputs become costly.
Example
In a 10-person meeting, everyone glancing at everyone is 100 glance-pairs; in a 1,000-person meeting it's a million. Doubling the people quadruples the glances — which is why "let everything see everything" is brilliant for capturing connections but punishing on very long inputs.

When the goal is to create, not classify

Most models are discriminative — they sort inputs into categories. Generative models instead learn what the data looks like well enough to produce convincing new examples of it. That shift changes how you judge success: there's no single right answer to check against.

Example
A discriminative model is an art critic who says "this is a Van Gogh." A generative model is a forger who paints a brand-new canvas in Van Gogh's style. Judging the critic is easy (right or wrong); judging the forger needs two questions — does each painting look real, and is there genuine variety, not the same canvas over and over?

These extensions share one theme: take the familiar network and adapt it to a new shape of problem — time, sequence, or creation. The most direct extension of what you already know is the first: keep the image-style filter, but let it scan a third axis such as time or depth. That's the three-dimensional CNN — a small step from ordinary image filters, with one big caveat about when the extra cost is actually worth it.
